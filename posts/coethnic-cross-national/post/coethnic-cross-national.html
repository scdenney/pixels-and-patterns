Which nation is the exception depends on the estimator

On the average, Taiwan is the low outlier and Korea looks like Germany. On the distribution, Korea is the high outlier and Germany looks like Taiwan. Only the second reading matches the histories.

By Steven Denney · Pixels and Patterns

A recent paper by Acharya, Hainmueller, and Xu starts from an observation about what a conjoint experiment can measure. The choices respondents make carry information about the whole distribution of how intensely they weigh an attribute, and the mean is only one summary of that distribution. The summary the field reports almost by reflex, the average marginal component effect, is an intensity-weighted average, so two populations can return the same number while differing sharply in how strongly, and how widely, a preference is held.

This post applies that observation to national-identity conjoints fielded in Germany, South Korea, and Taiwan, where the stakes are substantive and the average does more than blur. Making a candidate co-ethnic has a small positive effect on being chosen in all three countries, and on the average that effect is largest in Korea and Germany and smallest in Taiwan, so the usual summary makes Taiwan the exception and treats Korea and Germany as much the same. Read as a distribution of intensity, the picture inverts. Korea separates from the other two while Germany and Taiwan fall together, so the exceptional case is Korea, not Taiwan. The second reading is the one the histories predict.

Three nations that diverged

Germany and South Korea began modern nationhood in nearly the same place, and both built it on descent. German citizenship ran on jus sanguinis, membership inherited by blood, codified in the 1913 nationality law and carried well into the twentieth century, while Korean nationhood was organized around minjok (민족), a single ethnic nation defined by shared ancestry and a common bloodline. Compared at mid-century, the two would have looked similar, with ancestry near the center of each.

The paths then diverged. Germany, after 1945 and above all with the citizenship reform of 2000, gradually loosened the tie between nationhood and ethnic descent, adding elements of jus soli and grounding membership more in civic and constitutional attachment. South Korea, if anything, went the other way. The ethnic conception of the nation remained entrenched through democratization and, on some accounts, fused with democratic obligation.

Taiwan reached a civic identity by a third route. Over a generation of democratization its national identity was actively re-made against a Chinese ethnic frame, so that being Taiwanese came to rest less on descent, which the island shares with the mainland, than on a chosen attachment to the place and its institutions. Where Korea holds descent tightly and Germany has loosened it, Taiwan has the least reason of the three to make ancestry the test of belonging. The three now spread across the civic and ethnic distinction, Korea at the ethnic end and Germany and Taiwan toward the civic, and that spread is what the average cannot register in these data while the distribution can.

Why the average is built to hide intensity

A conjoint experiment randomizes the attributes of hypothetical profiles and asks respondents to choose between them, and in principle the design identifies both the trade-offs people make and the distribution of preferences across a population. In practice the field almost always reports a single number, the average marginal component effect, or AMCE, which is the effect of changing one attribute from a baseline to a comparison level on the probability that a profile is chosen, averaged over the other attributes and over respondents. The randomization identifies it, and it stays stable across reasonable task counts, which makes it the right first thing to compute.

The difficulty is that the AMCE answers a narrower question than it is often asked to answer. Because it averages over respondents and weights them by the intensity of their preferences, a positive and statistically clear AMCE need not mean that most people prefer the attribute. A committed minority, weighted by intensity, can produce the positive average while the majority stays divided. The quantity it identifies is an effect on a bounded choice probability, one step removed from the parameters of the underlying preference distribution, and a modest, similar AMCE across cases is consistent with distributions that differ in the way that matters here. The average compresses into one number the information that separates a preference held strongly and widely from one held weakly and narrowly.

Getting at that information means estimating the model the AMCE summarizes instead of stopping at the summary. In a random-utility model each respondent has a utility function over the attributes and chooses the higher-utility profile, and the quantity of interest becomes the distribution of the utility weight on a given attribute, which a mixed logit estimates directly. This is the structural object that Acharya, Hainmueller, and Xu generalize, in their case by letting a neural network make the mean preference vary with respondent characteristics and by learning the residual spread from repeated choices. I use a simpler and more transparent version of the same idea, a regularized hierarchical-Bayes mixed logit, which is enough to show the intensity contrast and which their estimator would refine.

Turning the identity battery into a conjoint

National identity is an unusually good place to run this comparison, because its standard instrument has a well-known ceiling. The national-identity battery of the International Social Survey Programme asks respondents to rate how important each criterion is for being truly a member of the nation, and people rate almost every criterion as important, so the battery records a plateau, not a ranking. Ask directly, and most Germans, Koreans, and Taiwanese will call ancestry important, and language too, without saying which criteria they would give up to keep the others.

The conjoint used here embeds that battery into a forced choice on purpose. Respondents weigh ancestry against language, residence, citizenship, tradition, institutions, and a sense of national feeling, all varying at once, and then choose a whole person, so the trade-off the standard instrument cannot reach becomes the quantity being measured. The conjoint operationalization of the identity battery is itself recent, and this post extends it to a three-country comparison.1 The three surveys share a common eight-attribute instrument, fielded in Germany in 2023 and in South Korea and Taiwan across 2023 and 2024, each carrying a co-ethnic ancestry marker, with analytic samples of 2,079, 2,000, and 2,050 respondents.2 The focal attribute throughout is ancestry, co-ethnic versus not.

Estimating the distribution of intensity

The estimand is the distribution of the co-ethnic utility weight across respondents, and the estimator is a regularized hierarchical-Bayes mixed logit with random coefficients on all eight attributes. The regularization is essential. With only four to six choice tasks per person, the individual slope on any one attribute is weakly identified, and an unregularized maximum-likelihood fit collapses the ancestry variance onto a singular boundary. Weakly informative priors hold the estimate off that boundary and return the remaining uncertainty as a posterior rather than a false point estimate.

From the fitted mean and spread of the ancestry weight, three quantities follow. The population-mean weight measures the average strength of the co-ethnic preference on the utility (logit) scale, which puts it on the same footing as the other seven attributes. The prefer-and-reject split is the model's estimate of the share of the population whose individual weight is positive, which registers how widely the preference is held. The intensity shares are the fractions placing at least moderate (above 0.5) or strong (above 1) weight on ancestry, which registers how deeply. Each comes with a credible interval, and I read the last two as corroborating the ordering, without decimal precision.3

On the AMCE, the co-ethnic effect is positive everywhere and cleanly ordered. It is largest in South Korea at 0.082 and close behind in Germany at 0.070. Taiwan sits lower at 0.042. The Korea and Germany figures are not distinguishable from each other, their gap of 0.012 carrying a standard error of 0.010, while both sit clearly above Taiwan. So the average makes Taiwan the low outlier and Korea and Germany near-twins, treating the ethnic case and the civic case as much the same (Figure 1).

The structural read rearranges the three. The co-ethnic utility weight is 0.69 in South Korea, well above 0.48 in Germany and 0.43 in Taiwan, and the two lower cases are not distinguishable from each other, their difference of 0.05 carrying a credible interval that runs through zero. Korea separates cleanly, the Korea and Germany gap of 0.21 on the structural scale carrying a 95 percent credible interval of [0.06, 0.38] and a posterior probability of about 0.997 that Koreans weigh ancestry more heavily. On the average the exceptional case was Taiwan, on the distribution it is Korea, and Germany has changed partners, indistinguishable from Korea on the average and from Taiwan on the distribution.

The reason the two readings diverge is scale. The structural weight is an unbounded utility parameter, while the AMCE is a difference of bounded choice probabilities, and the bounded scale compresses a wide range of utilities into a narrow band before any averaging begins. Between two candidates identical on everything but ancestry, the average Korean chooses the co-ethnic one about 67 percent of the time, against 62 percent in Germany and 61 percent in Taiwan, so bounding alone has already flattened sizeable differences in utility to a few points of choice probability. Averaging over every randomized context and every respondent then takes the rest, down to the AMCE gaps the table reports. The compression regroups the cases. The pair it treats as alike is not the pair the utilities treat as alike.

The distribution also says how each population divides, because the model estimates the spread of individual weights along with the mean. On breadth the three are close. The fitted distribution puts about four in five respondents on the positive side of ancestry in every country, 81 percent in South Korea and 77 in Germany, with Taiwan between them at 79. The separation is in depth. Korea puts far more of its mass at the demanding end, 59 percent placing at least moderate weight on ancestry against 48 percent in Germany and 44 in Taiwan, and 34 percent placing strong weight against 21 and 15. Korea's distribution sits farthest to the right, its upper tail the heaviest, while Germany and Taiwan crowd together nearer the center (Figure 2).

The direct battery, for all its ceiling, points the same way. In each country the conjoint preference for a co-ethnic profile rises, broadly, with the respondent's stated importance of ancestry, so the forced choice and the direct item track the same construct (Figure 3). And the battery reproduces the structural ordering on its own terms, with 60 percent of Koreans rating ancestry important against 40 percent of Germans and 34 percent of Taiwanese, and Korea again stands apart from a Germany and Taiwan that sit close together.

Taiwan's low weight on ancestry is not indifference to national belonging. In Taiwan the co-ethnic marker is Han descent, the very ancestry the island shares with the mainland, so a light weight on it is the civic-national move rather than the absence of one. And the same conjoints that rank ancestry lowest in Taiwan rank a felt sense of national belonging highest of any criterion in the study, higher there than in Korea or Germany. Taiwan asks little about where a person comes from and much about whether they feel Taiwanese.

Is the structural read worth it?

Not always, and it helps to be clear about when it is not. If the estimand is the population-average causal effect of an attribute, which is the standard design-based question, the AMCE is the appropriate tool and the mixed logit is overkill. The structural read earns its added machinery only when the question is distributional, when what matters is how strongly a preference is held and how widely it is shared. National identity is squarely that kind of question, which is why the average here answers a narrower question than the cases pose.

When the question is distributional, the payoff is not only precision. Here the average and the distribution disagree about which country is exceptional. The average makes Taiwan the outlier and Korea and Germany near-twins, which reverses the contrast the histories lead you to expect, the textbook ethnic case and the textbook civic case rendered alike. The distribution puts the exception back where it belongs. Korea separates from both, consistent with an ethnic conception of nationhood that held firm under democratization, while Germany and Taiwan fall together, a post-descent Germany and a Taiwan built against a shared ancestry meeting from opposite directions at a low weight on descent. The average blurs this contrast and locates the exception in the wrong country.

The structural read also has a clear limit. A design with four to six tasks per respondent pins down population averages well but leaves the upper tail of the individual distribution, the share who hold the preference strongly, weakly identified, so the ordering is well estimated even where the precise size of the tail is not. The point is visible in Taiwan, whose intensity shares carry the widest intervals of the three even though its sample is the largest, a weaker and less consensual signal rather than less data. Within a fielded survey the task count is fixed, so sharpening the tail means drawing information from somewhere else. Acharya, Hainmueller, and Xu do this by modeling the mean preference as a function of observed respondent characteristics while still learning the residual spread from repeated choices, so that each individual estimate draws on variation across the whole sample as well as that respondent's own handful of choices. The direct battery used here, the stated importance of ancestry above all, is the obvious characteristic to index on, and that estimator is the natural next step from the analysis reported here.
